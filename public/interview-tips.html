Interview tips

Walk in knowing exactly what they're looking for.

The same interview, three different worlds. Pick your track and learn how each panel really evaluates you — and how to answer.

The core idea

Past behaviour is the best predictor of future performance.

Across the UN system, almost every question is about something you actually did. Show the right competency, with depth, and what you learned.

45–60 minlength
4–5questions
5–8 minper answer

During the interview

Do
Talk in "I", not "we" — your role, actions and impact.
Stand up on phone/video; use pitch, tone, speed, silence.
It's fine to talk about challenges — show what you learned.
Prepare one good question for the panel.
Don't
Answer in the hypothetical or in vague generalities.
Overstate your role — panels probe and references validate.
Interrupt the panel.
Ask about salary or benefits (it's on the website).

For human-rights roles

Speak the language of the mandate

For OHCHR and similar mandates

Weave the international human-rights framework into your examples. Whenever you give a STAR/CAR-L answer, name the mechanism it connected to.

Special proceduresSpecial Rapporteurs, Working Groups, Independent Experts — how your work fed their mandates.
Treaty bodiesCCPR, CESCR, CEDAW, CAT, CRC, CERD, CRPD — reporting, follow-up to concluding observations.
Conventions & instrumentsGround examples in specific conventions and standards, not generic "human rights".
Groups in vulnerabilityRefugees, IDPs, migrants, women, children, persons with disabilities, indigenous peoples, LGBTI.
Example move: instead of "I coordinated a report," say "I coordinated inputs that fed into the Special Rapporteur's country report, aligned with the State's CEDAW concluding observations."
EPSO — 2023 framework

Structured, competency-based — eight general competencies.

After EPSO's tests or a CAST shortlisting, the interview mixes a short self-introduction, technical questions on your policy area, and competency questions — with STAR as the expected structure.

3 (up to 5)panellists
30–45 minlength
~10 minper competency
2–3 minintro pitch

How the interview flows

1

Introduce yourself

A 2–3 min elevator pitch anchored to the vacancy's Essential Requirements.

2

Technical questions

Your policy area: the relevant EU framework, regulation or programme.

3

Competency questions

1–3 behavioural questions, answered with STAR, from the Notice of Competition.

4

Soft-skills

Often in a 2nd EU working language (French/German).

The eight general competencies

EPSO Competency Framework
Critical thinking & creative problem-solving Decision-making & getting results Information management (digital & data) Self-management Working together Learning as a skill Communication Intrapreneurship
Pro tip

Not all eight are tested every time. Check the Notice of Competition — it names which competencies your panel will focus on. Each one breaks down into "anchors" (specific behaviours the panel scores).

During the interview

Do
Prepare the opening pitch — it's predictable, so rehearse it.
Look up your panel on LinkedIn (names come in the invite).
Show, don't tell — give evidence, not "I'm a great team player".
Be concise — long answers work against you.
Don't
Start with personal history ("I was born in…").
Sound over-rehearsed or memorized.
Claim you need no further learning — a self-awareness red flag.
Give abstract statements with no concrete example.

The differentiator

Your origin story

A 2–3 minute story that changes how they see you

Storytelling is one of the most influential forms of communication. Don't be the person who describes themselves with adjectives — be the one who differentiates themselves with a short, true story tied directly to the job.

Build it: a turning point → what you did → the result it created → why it points straight at this role. Rehearse it until it sounds natural, not memorized.

Coming from the UN / NGO world?

Translate your impact into business language

The same achievements, re-registered for a corporate panel. Lead with outcomes and metrics, and drop institutional jargon.
